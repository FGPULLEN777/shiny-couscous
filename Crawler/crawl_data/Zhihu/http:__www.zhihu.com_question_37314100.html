    As a social-scientist-in-training studying political economy, my academic interest directs me to this ongoing policy debate. With a close examination of the policy proposal and the works it refers to, I believe that the proposed new rule of OPT extension is well reasoned and that the policy will yield net positive effect on the US economy and the society. This review consists of the following parts. First, I will elaborate why I believe that the policy has positive long-term effect. Second, I will discuss why I see no negative short-term effect. In the section I especially address two criticisms of the policy in previous comments, one on “waste of tax money” and one on “unemployment of US workers”. As you will see, I try to put forward my arguments with a balanced consideration of the domestic economy and the international competition that the US economy is facing in this increasing globalized world.

    The Long-term Effect

    First of all, I believe that the policy has a net long-term benefit on the US economy and society overall. The foreign STEM students, once allowed to be enrolled in this training program after graduation, contribute to the US economy by (1) increasing the human capital and (2) “spilling over” knowledge at their workplace with their colleagues. These two points follow the Endogenous Growth Theory by macroeconomist Paul Romer, which argues that investment in human capacity and innovation can boost economic growth of a country. In this sense, The OPT Extension policy is very cost-effective for the country. As we notice, the foreign STEM students invest in their own education with money from their home country (though not the focus of my discussion here, I should point out this itself brings benefit the US economy, as the policy proposal correctly points out on page 63382). The policy choice is clear enough with this setup. With the new OPT Extension policy, the US gains an increase of Human Capital for free. The foreign STEM students gain further training at work, and at the same time they contribute add to the human capital of their industries, spilling over their knowledge and skills at their workplace when they collaborate with their colleagues.

    It is especially worth pointing out that the American STEM workers are also beneficiaries of the policy, as they can potentially improve their technical and leadership skills by collaborating and communicating with their foreign colleagues. This argument is grounded on solid evidence. As the policy proposal points out, an alumni survey of Duke University in 2013 shows that foreign students increase academic exchange in US campuses, through which increases self-evaluated skill development of students. The foreign student, once allowed to be trained in STEM industries, will do the same good at their workplace. The skills that foreign STEM students can help US workers develop, I believe, is increasingly valuable for American STEM workers and managers when the US companies are reaching out to the emerging markets and facing intense competitions from their counterparts in other countries. The study of Giovanni Peri, Kevin Shih and Chad Sparber (2014) cited by the policy proposal provides indirect evidence. It shows that the number of STEM workers is positively associated with the salaries of native STEM workers and has no or positive correlation with non-STEM workers.

    To sum up my argument about the long-term effect, I believe that the new policy has net long-term benefit on the US economy. This long-term effect should be expected not only on the macro economy, but can also be spelled out with regards of skill formation of American STEM workers.

    Short-term effect

    Moving to my second point, I see no reason to expect negative short-term effect from the policy, upon reviewing previous researches (including and beyond what the policy proposal has cited) and the design of the policy itself. This part is formulated as a response to many previous criticisms.

    The first common criticism claims that the “program is a waste of taxpayers’ money”. I argue that the program contributes to to the fiscal strength of the US and help the low-income families in the country. Before making my argument, I would like to add a side note that this type of reasoning also appears in the media. I see similar argument on a few news report, for instance, “OPT program offers businesses incentive to hire foreign workers over Americans, critics say” by Stephen Dinan of the Washington Times. My response to critics is that, criticisms taking this view unintentionally or intentionally miss this common sensual knowledge, that foreign STEM students pay tax! Quite to the opposite of this criticism, they are, once allowed to stay for this work-training program, going to contribute to the fiscal strength of the United states. What is more, as it is reasonable to believe that the salaries of the foreign STEM students are above the median income and the US tax system is progressive, their stay helps the low-income families in the US by paying taxing to finance the social welfare system. To sum up, the new OPT extension rule increases tax to the US and help the poor because the students who stay pay a considerable amount of tax.

    The second common criticism claims that the new policy allows foreign STEM students to take away jobs of US workers. This point, though politically appealing, is entirely ungrounded given previous researches and the design of this policy. I put forward three arguments. First, the discussion about the possible “substitution” effect on the labor market is meaningless without clearly defining “who takes away whose job”. Second, I argue that foreign STEM students cannot possibly leads to unemployment of their fellow American STEM workers. Third, I argue that foreign STEM students actually create non-STEM jobs.

    To start the discussion, I would like to first point out that it makes sense to say that foreign STEM students threaten American workers only in one circumstance, that is, the stay of the foreign student under the new policy causes the unemployment of a *similarly situated* American workers. Any argument attempting to prove such negative effect need to provide evidence that the American workers “replaced” by foreign worker is “similarly situated”. Unfortunately, few, if any, of the anecdotes I see in the comment submitted previously attempt to prove this.

    Moving to my second point, I argue that the job of American STEM workers cannot possibly be threatened by the new OTP extension policy for two reasons – (1) the industry is booming and face a short of labor supply; (2) the policy is designed in a way that protects American STEM workers. First, the STEM industry of the US is booming, making the stay of foreign student a win-win game, instead of a zero-sum game for the native workers. Simply put, the foreign student does not take away anyone’s job because the labor market is short of supply. Though the boom of the industry is commonsensical enough, I would like to elaborate the fact with the specific example of software engineering. According to an industry report by Alex Silady published this April (“The Rise of the Software Engineer”, URL: https://smartasset.com/insights/the-rise-of-the-software-engineer), the job opening of software engineering has increased by 50% in the past decade, while its wage is twice of median salary of the US. Its unemployment rate of is only half of the overall rate. Looking forward, the Bureau of Labor Statistics forecast that the demand of software develop will grow by 22% by 2022, an increase much faster than average. Regardless this increasing demand, American college students are no more likely to choose computer science as their major in recent years, according to the data reported by NPR (“What’s your major? 4 Decades of college degrees, in 1 Graph”, URL: http://www.npr.org/sections/money/2014/05/09/310114739/whats-your-major-four-decades-of-college-degrees-in-1-graph). The mismatch of the change in labor supply and demand leaves a gap for foreign students to fill in, which benefits the industry without harming native workers.

    The second reason why the proposed policy does not harm local STEM workers is that the proposed policy includes well-specified terms protecting local workers (section G. from page 63388 to 63390). This part of the policy requires that foreign STEM OPT students be compensated in a way that commensurate with their US counterparts and that US workers not be laid off because the entry of foreign workers. I put forward to comments here. First, the regulation on equal compensation is ethical and fair for both foreign OPT students and US workers. This protect both groups from unethical exploitation from the firm and guarantee their equal opportunities. Second, the assurance that the employment of foreign students does not lead to unemployment of native workers is somewhat redundant, given the excess demand in the industry as laid out above, but it is acceptable and it provides a double-insurance for US STEM workers.

    My final comment about the short-term effect of the proposed policy regards its effect on non-STEM jobs. I argue that the prospective participants of the proposed OPT extension program creates non-STEM jobs because of their consumption. Here I put forward an argument with economic intuition and back it with some evidence. Foreign STEM students who stay for another 24 months due to the policy have two charaters that shape their preferences of consumption: (1) they are above the median of the income scale; (2) they have short or medium-length time horizon as residents of the US. This means they have money to spend and they are more inclined to prioritize consumption over investment than the people with longer time horizon. As it follows, the participants of the STEM OPT extension policies have the capability and motivation to spend on consumer goods in the United States. The money, of course, flows into American companies and households that are part of the provision of goods and services. I argue that, as a result, it creates non-STEM and make people already on those jobs better off. The evidence of this argument comes straight out of the policy proposal. First, as the most direct evidence, the NBER paper by Giovanni Peri et al (2014), to re-iterate, finds positive effect of STEM worker inflow on non-STEM workers’ salary in some places. Also, the paper finds a positive correlation between rent and inflow of STEM workers, meaning that the workers benefit American property owners by paying their rents. Second, the points the policy proposal makes about how foreign students contribute to local communities with their tuition and consumption applies the same way for the graduates who participate in the proposed program. Third, friendly comments from the neighborhood on this board gives me some anecdotal evidence (though in no way systematic robust) that the foreign students have positive effect on the non-STEM communities.

    As a final note, I will re-evaluate all the above comment with the consideration of globalization and international competition. One potential objection to my argument that foreign students does not take away jobs of local workers is that, the increased labor supply does decrease their salaries. That is, with a further labor shortage if the United States does not implement the proposed policy, say, native software developers would receive higher wage. My response is two fold. First, I do not think the marginal increase, if any, is large. Second and much more importantly, the STEM industries of the US face global competition and the competitors are catching up. If the foreign students go back to their home countries without the program, they are going to compete with their US counterparts anyway. This scenario is even less desirable, since the US firms do not enjoy the benefit they bring, and their US colleagues do not benefit from the knowledge spillover they bring about (as discussed in the previous section).
